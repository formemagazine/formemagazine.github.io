Giorgio Armani, the man who softened fashion

Before Giorgio Armani, power dressing was loud.

The 1970s and early ’80s were defined by heavy structures, sharp tailoring, aggressive shoulders, and commanding colors. Fashion functioned as armor — strength was built directly into the garment.

Then Armani shifted the narrative.

He removed internal structures. Reduced padding. Softened silhouettes. In doing so, he redefined power — not as force, but as control.

His wool blazers appeared structured yet fluid. Matte finishes replaced shine. Neutrals replaced excess. Authority no longer needed volume to be visible.

Then came silk.

Traditional tailoring emphasized rigidity. Armani introduced movement. His silk blazers draped instead of constrained, creating motion rather than angles.

Compared to designers embracing spectacle and drama, Armani chose restraint. Where others sculpted exaggeration, he sculpted precision.

Before Armani, tailoring imposed structure.

After Armani, tailoring followed the body.
